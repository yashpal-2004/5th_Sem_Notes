Probability Foundations

1.1 Why Probability in Deep Learning?

Machine learning models must make decisions under uncertainty. Probability theory provides a formal framework to model and reason about incomplete information. In deep learning, uncertainty arises from three distinct sources:

Three Sources of Uncertainty
  1. Incomplete Modeling: Occurs when we discard details for simplicity (Jo abhi jaan-na nahi chahte). For example, a coin toss can be predicted deterministically using aerodynamics and wind force, but we treat it as random to simplify the model.
  2. Incomplete Observability: Occurs when we cannot observe all variables (Jo abhi nahi jaante). For example, estimating the health of a second-hand car/bike/phone without seeing its complete internal usage history.
  3. Inherent Stochasticity: Occurs when the system itself is fundamentally random (Jo jaana nahi jaa sakta). For example, the behavior of subatomic particles in quantum mechanics.

1.2 Probability Terminology

To mathematically model random processes, we define standard building blocks:

  • Random Experiment: A process that leads to one of several possible outcomes, where the exact outcome is uncertain beforehand (e.g., rolling a fair 6-sided die).
  • Outcomes: The individual results of an experiment (e.g., landing on 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6).
  • Sample Space (S): The collection of all possible outcomes (e.g., S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}).
  • Events: A subset of the sample space containing one or more outcomes (e.g., getting an even number, E = {2, 4, 6}).
  • Probability of an Event P(E): A value from 0 to 1 indicating the likelihood of that event. For example, P(rolling an even number) = |{2, 4, 6}| / |{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}| = 3/6 = 0.5.

1.3 Joint & Conditional Probability

In deep learning, we typically analyze multiple random variables simultaneously:

  • Random Variable (X): A variable whose value is determined by the outcome of a random experiment.
  • Joint Probability P(X=x, Y=y): The probability that event X equals x AND event Y equals y simultaneously.
  • Conditional Probability P(A|B): The probability of event A occurring given that event B has already occurred:
    P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)   [where P(B) > 0]
  • Chain Rule of Probability: Joint probabilities can be decomposed into a product of several conditional probabilities:
    P(A, B) = P(A|B)P(B)
    For n variables, the chain rule generalizes to:
    P(X1, ..., Xn) = ∏i=1n P(Xi | X1, ..., Xi-1)

1.4 Bayes' Theorem & Diagnosis

Bayes' Theorem updates the probability of a hypothesis (A) based on new evidence (B):

Bayes' Theorem Formula
P(A|B) = ( P(B|A) × P(A) ) / P(B)

Where:

  • P(A): Prior probability (initial belief in hypothesis A).
  • P(B|A): Likelihood (probability of seeing evidence B if hypothesis A is true).
  • P(B): Marginal evidence (overall probability of seeing evidence B).
  • P(A|B): Posterior probability (updated belief in A after seeing evidence B).
Example: Medical Diagnosis

Suppose 2% of the population has Typhoid (Prior: P(A) = 0.02). The natural rate of fever in the population is 5% (Evidence: P(B) = 0.05). If someone has Typhoid, their chance of having a fever is 50% (Likelihood: P(B|A) = 0.50).

What is the probability that someone with a fever has Typhoid?

P(Typhoid | Fever) = ( P(Fever | Typhoid) × P(Typhoid) ) / P(Fever)

P(A|B) = (0.50 × 0.02) / 0.05 = 0.01 / 0.05 = 0.20 (20%)

Expectation & Statistical Metrics

2.1 Expected Value (Expectation)

The Expected Value of a random variable is the probability-weighted average of all its possible values over many trials. For a discrete random variable X with values xi and probabilities pi:

E[X] = ∑ xi pi

2.2 Variance & Covariance

To analyze the distribution spread and relationships between variables, we use variance and covariance:

  • Variance Var(X): Measures the dispersion of a random variable around its mean. It indicates how much the values fluctuate:
    Var(X) = E[(X - E[X])2] = E[X2] - (E[X])2
  • Covariance Cov(X, Y): Measures the linear relationship between two random variables:
    Cov(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])]
    • Positive Covariance: X and Y tend to increase or decrease together.
    • Negative Covariance: When X increases, Y tends to decrease, and vice versa.
    • Zero Covariance: No linear association exists between X and Y.

2.3 Correlation

Covariance is scale-dependent. To get a standardized measure of the linear relationship between variables, we use the correlation coefficient (ρ), which restricts values to the range [-1, 1]:

ρX,Y = Cov(X, Y) / ( σX × σY )

Where σX and σY are the standard deviations of X and Y respectively.

Classification Paradigms & LLMs

3.1 Discriminative vs. Generative Models

In machine learning classification, algorithms are split into two primary paradigms:

Paradigm Comparison
  • Discriminative Models: Learn the decision boundary directly by estimating the conditional probability P(Class | Evidence) from the training data.
    • Goal: Predict the class label of new data points based on feature boundaries.
    • Examples: Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machines (SVMs), Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs).
    • Observation: Discriminative models make predictions using patterns without needing to understand the underlying distribution of the input features.
  • Generative Models: Estimate the joint probability distribution P(Class, Evidence) of the training data and labels.
    • Goal: Model the complete distribution of the data. This allows sampling/generating new instances distributed identically to the training set.
    • Examples: Naive Bayes, Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), GANs, Variational Autoencoders (VAEs).

3.2 Language Models as Generative Systems

Large Language Models (LLMs) operate as generative sequence models by predicting the probability of the next word given a context of preceding words:

  • Sample Space: The entire set of words in the model's vocabulary.
  • Autoregressive Factoring: Using the chain rule of conditional probability, the overall probability of a sentence is computed as: 
    P(w1, w2, w3, ..., wn) = P(w1) × P(w2|w1) × P(w3|w1,w2) × ... × P(wn|w1,...,wn-1)
Real-world Context Counts: Emma by Jane Austen

Consider estimating word probabilities sequentially in a text corpus:

  • Prior Probability P(word): The general probability of seeing a word in isolation (e.g., common words like "the", "and").
  • Conditional Probability P(word | "I"): The probability of the next word given the context "I".
  • P(word | "I have"): The context narrows down the likely candidates.
  • P(word | "I have no"): The context creates a highly specific distribution focusing on nouns (like "idea", "doubt").

Information Theory & Divergences

4.1 Information Content & Surprise

Information theory formalizes the concept of "surprise." An event with a high probability of occurring provides very little information when it happens. Conversely, a low-probability event provides significant new information (high surprise):

I(xi) = -log2(pi)

Where pi is the probability of outcome xi. The unit of measurement is the bit (the information gained from a single fair coin flip, where p = 0.5 and I(x) = -log2(0.5) = 1 bit).

4.2 Shannon Entropy

The Entropy H(X) of an experiment is the expected value of the information content across all possible outcomes. It measures the average uncertainty of the random variable:

H(X) = - ∑ pi log2(pi)
Example: Biased Coin Flip

Consider a coin with a 90% chance of landing on Heads (p = 0.9) and 10% on Tails (p = 0.1):

  • Information in Heads: I(Heads) = -log2(0.9) ≈ 0.152 bits
  • Information in Tails: I(Tails) = -log2(0.1) ≈ 3.322 bits
  • Entropy H(X): 0.9 × 0.152 + 0.1 × 3.322 ≈ 0.469 bits

Since the outcome is highly predictable, the average uncertainty (0.469 bits) is much lower than that of a fair coin (1.00 bit).

4.3 Cross-Entropy Loss

When modeling, we estimate a target distribution P using an estimated distribution Q. The Cross-Entropy H(P, Q) measures the average surprise when we receive events from true distribution P but evaluate them under our assumed distribution Q:

H(P, Q) = - ∑ pi log2(qi)
Example: Deceived Coin Toss

Suppose you are given a 90% biased coin (true P: heads = 0.9, tails = 0.1) but are told it is a fair coin (assumed Q: heads = 0.5, tails = 0.5):

  • Your assumed surprise for Heads: -log2(0.5) = 1.0 bit
  • Your assumed surprise for Tails: -log2(0.5) = 1.0 bit
  • Expected surprise (Cross-Entropy): (0.9 × 1.0) + (0.1 × 1.0) = 1.0 bit

Because the estimate was incorrect, the expected surprise (1.00 bit) is higher than the true entropy (0.469 bits). This excess surprise forms the basis of Cross-Entropy Loss in classification training.

4.4 Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence

The KL Divergence measures the exact amount of additional surprise introduced by estimating P using Q. It represents the distance between the two distributions:

DKL(P || Q) = H(P, Q) - H(P) = ∑ pi log2( pi / qi )
KL Divergence and Asymmetry

For the deceived coin example above:

DKL(P || Q) = 1.00 - 0.469 = 0.531 bits

Asymmetry Warning: KL divergence is not a true distance metric because it is asymmetric. The direction matters:

DKL(P || Q) ≠ DKL(Q || P)
