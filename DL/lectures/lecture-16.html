Tokenization Strategies

1.1 Character vs. Word Tokenization

Tokenization is the first step in NLP, breaking raw text into digestible units. We evaluate the trade-offs of different approaches:

  • Character-Level: Treats each letter as a token.
    • Pros: Small vocabulary size, robust to typos, and eliminates out-of-vocabulary (OOV) issues.
    • Cons: Greatly increases sequence length, raising computational costs and training complexity. It also struggles to capture higher-level semantic concepts.
  • Word-Level: Treats each unique word as a token.
    • Pros: Efficiently maps distinct semantic concepts as individual units.
    • Cons: Requires massive vocabularies, cannot handle morphological variations, and struggles with out-of-vocabulary words.

1.2 n-gram Contextual Tokenization

n-grams group n consecutive words into a single token (e.g. unigram "good", bigram "not good"). This preserves local context and phrase meaning. It is particularly useful for distinguishing negations from root words, preventing opposite meanings from collapsing into the same vector space coordinates. However, n-grams lead to a combinatorial vocabulary explosion.

Discrete Sparse Representations

2.1 One-Hot Encoding Limitations

Early NLP models represented words as discrete symbols. One-Hot Encoding creates a vector of vocabulary size |V|, setting a 1 at the word's unique index and 0 elsewhere. This approach faces two key limitations:

  1. Storage and Scaling (Sparsity): A vocabulary of 50,000 words yields vectors with 50,000 dimensions, where 99.9% of the cells are zero. This is highly inefficient.
  2. The Similarity Problem (Orthogonality): The dot product between any two distinct one-hot vectors is exactly zero. The model has no geometric way to recognize that "king" and "queen" are semantically closer than "king" and "table".

2.2 Bag of Words (BoW) Models

To represent complete documents, the Bag-of-Words model sums the one-hot vectors of all words in the text, creating a fixed-size vector of vocabulary length |V| containing word counts. While simple and fast, BoW has major drawbacks: it completely ignores word order and grammar, and fails to capture semantic meaning (e.g. "I like AI" and "AI likes me" yield identical representations).

2.3 TF-IDF Term Weighting Formula

To address the issue of common words (like "the", "is") dominating frequency counts, TF-IDF reweights BoW vectors by multiplying a term's local frequency by its global rarity across the corpus:

TF-IDF Math
  • Term Frequency (TF): How often a term appears in the current document.
  • Inverse Document Frequency (IDF): Measures term uniqueness across all N documents:
    IDF = log( N / Docs with Term )
  • TF-IDF Score: TF × IDF. High scores highlight terms that are frequent in a specific document but rare across the corpus, dimming common words.

Word2Vec: CBOW Architecture

3.1 Predicting Target from Context

Mikolov et al. (Google, 2013) introduced Word2Vec, transitioning from word counting to prediction-based models based on the Distributional Hypothesis (words used in similar contexts share similar meanings). The Continuous Bag of Words (CBOW) architecture predicts a missing target word from its surrounding context words:

  • Input Layer: One-hot vectors for context words within a sliding window.
  • Hidden Layer (Projection): Computes the linear average of the context word vectors. Crucially, there is no non-linear activation function.
  • Output Layer: Applies a softmax function to generate a probability distribution over the vocabulary.

3.2 The Softmax Computational Bottleneck

Evaluating the softmax function at each step requires computing an exponent and summing over the entire vocabulary |V|:

P(w | context) = exp(v′wT vcontext) / ∑u ∈ V exp(v′uT vcontext)

For large vocabularies (e.g. |V| = 100,000+), computing this denominator at every iteration is highly expensive, creating a severe training bottleneck.

4

Word2Vec: Skip-gram Architecture

4.1 Predicting Context from Target

The Skip-gram architecture flips the CBOW task: it takes a single target word and predicts the surrounding context words. While slower to train, Skip-gram excels at representing rare words because it receives multiple training signals for each target word instance.

4.2 Optimization via Negative Sampling

To solve the softmax bottleneck, Word2Vec uses Negative Sampling (SGNS). Instead of predicting a target word from the entire vocabulary, the model frames optimization as binary logistic regression:

  • For each positive word pair (target, context), the model updates the weights to maximize their similarity.
  • It also samples a small set of k random "negative" words from a noise distribution (typically k ∈ [5, 20]) and updates the weights to minimize their similarity.
  • This reduces the computational complexity from O(|V|) to O(k), making training on large vocabularies highly scalable.

GloVe Global Embeddings

5.1 Global Co-occurrence Matrix

While Word2Vec captures semantic details, it relies on local sliding windows, failing to exploit global corpus statistics. Conversely, count-based decomposition methods exploit global statistics but struggle with local context. 5.2 Log Co-occurrence Dot Product

GloVe optimizes word vectors such that their dot product approximates the log co-occurrence probability of the words:

wiT w̃j + bi + b̃j ≈ log( Xij )

Where wi and w̃j are the word vectors, and bi, b̃j are bias terms. Taking the logarithm converts multiplication into addition, improving numerical stability. This objective encodes semantic relationships directly into the geometry of the vector space, enabling vector arithmetic like King − Man + Woman ≈ Queen.
