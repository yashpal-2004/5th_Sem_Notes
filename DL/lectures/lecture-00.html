1.2 Moving from Statistics to Deep Learning

In previous courses like Probability & Statistics, we asked the question: "Given the data, what is the likelihood?" In Deep Learning, we shift from measuring uncertainty to modeling complexity.

The Mathematical Engine

Deep learning is not magic; it is an extension of foundational mathematics. The calculus, Bayes' Theorem, and probability distributions you mastered are the primary engines under the hood of every model we will build.

What is Deep Learning?

2.1 Representation Learning

The core distinction between traditional Machine Learning and Deep Learning lies in feature curation:

  • Traditional ML: Humans manually engineer and select features (e.g., color intensity, roundness, texture descriptors). The human tells the machine what to look for.
  • Representation Learning (DL): We do not design features; we design architectures that automatically learn features directly from raw data.

2.2 The Feature Hierarchy

Deep neural networks learn abstract representations through a nested hierarchy of concepts:

  1. Low-level features: Simple edges and color gradients.
  2. Mid-level features: Textures, corners, and basic shapes.
  3. High-level features: Complex objects (e.g., faces, wheels, text).
Key Insight: Optimization over Heuristics

By using deep representation learning, we replace human heuristics with mathematical optimization. The network independently discovers the optimal representations for the target task.

The Unsung Architect: AlexNet (2012)

3.1 The 2012 AI Big Bang

The year 2012 marked the "Big Bang" of modern Artificial Intelligence during the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC). Before 2012, computer vision progress was slow and incremental. In 2012, a deep convolutional neural network named AlexNet shattered all previous records.

ILSVRC 2012 Top-5 Error Rate
  • Runner-up (Traditional CV): 26.2% error rate
  • AlexNet (Deep Learning): 15.3% error rate

This massive gap was a paradigm shift that sent shockwaves through the scientific community, ushering in the Third Golden Age of AI.

3.2 Pioneers & Divergent Paths

The AlexNet breakthrough was created by a team at the University of Toronto: Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton. Following their ImageNet victory, their startup DNN Research Inc. was acquired by Google, and their paths began to diverge:

  • Geoffrey Hinton & Ilya Sutskever: Took public-facing leadership and media roles. Hinton became known as the "Godfather of AI" and a major voice on AI risk. Sutskever co-founded OpenAI in 2015, served as its Chief Scientist, and later co-founded Safe Superintelligence Inc. in 2024.
  • Alex Krizhevsky: Opted for a career focused purely on technical innovation, largely staying out of the public spotlight. He remained at Google until 2017, later joining smaller firms like Dessa and Two Bear Capital.

3.3 The Anonymity of Alex Krizhevsky

Despite being the first author of the paper and the primary architect who engineered the GPU code critical for training the massive model, Krizhevsky remains relatively unknown to the general public compared to his co-authors.

Reflections on Success

His story highlights fundamental questions about career success: Is it measured by public acclaim or by private impact? Is it better to be the face of a revolution, or the one building its engine? There is no right answer, but it shows a deliberate preference for deep technical work over the demands of management and public life.

The Bitter Lesson (Richard Sutton)

4.1 Human Knowledge vs. Raw Computation

In his famous essay, The Bitter Lesson, AI pioneer Richard Sutton outlines a core pattern in the history of AI research: general-purpose methods that leverage computation always defeat methods based on human-engineered domain knowledge.

The Two Paths of AI
  • The Path of Human Knowledge: Build in our own clever insights and domain-specific structures. This approach is intuitive and satisfying for the researcher in the short term, but it inevitably plateaus.
  • The Path of Raw Computation: Rely on general methods (search and learning) that scale directly with processing power. Let the machine discover its own representations.

4.3 Historic Case Studies

The lesson has repeated across multiple fields over seven decades:

  • Computer Chess: Researchers initially tried to encode grandmaster strategic knowledge (openings, endgames). In 1997, IBM's Deep Blue defeated Garry Kasparov using massive, brute-force search.
  • Computer Go: Go was believed to be too complex for pure search. Researchers spent decades encoding human positional heuristics. In 2016, AlphaGo defeated Lee Sedol by combining massive search with learning from self-play.
  • Speech Recognition: Early systems engineered vocal tract and phoneme models. They were easily outperformed by statistical methods (Hidden Markov Models) and later by deep neural networks that learned directly from raw audio waveforms.
  • Computer Vision: Hand-crafted feature descriptors (like SIFT, Gabor filters, and edge detectors) were abandoned once deep convolutional networks (like AlexNet) learned representations automatically.

4.3 Why the Lesson is Bitter

The lesson is "bitter" because it is a humbling one for researchers. We naturally want our own clever insights to be the key to machine intelligence. Accepting that simply scaling computation, search, and learning performs significantly better forces us to abandon our psychological commitment to building systems that "think the way we think."

Core Takeaways
  • The future belongs to general-purpose methods that scale with computation.
  • We must stop trying to hardcode the contents of our minds (space, objects, grammar) into agents.
  • We should focus on building meta-methods that enable machines to discover knowledge for themselves.

Course Architecture & Pillars

5.1 The Pillars: Math & Code

Success in this course requires mastering two interconnected pillars:

  • The Math (The Physics): Deep learning is calculus and linear algebra acting on data. We will open the "black box" and derive concepts like Backpropagation by hand so that when models fail, you understand the mathematical reason why.
  • The Code (The Engineering): We build what we derive. You will gain proficiency in PyTorch/TensorFlow by implementing computational graphs from scratch rather than just calling black-box library APIs.

5.2 Syllabus & Evaluation Details

The course is structured into five core modules:

  • Module 1: Core Probability Theory, Bayes' Rule, Information Theory, and Linear Algebra operations.
  • Module 2: Deep Feedforward Networks (XOR example, Hidden Units), Backpropagation mechanics, and Regularization.
  • Module 3: Optimization for deep models and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).
  • Module 4: Sequence Modeling, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs/LSTMs), NLP applications, and Autoencoder foundations.
  • Module 5: Advanced Autoencoders, Attention Mechanisms, Transformer Architecture, and Large Language Models (LLMs).
