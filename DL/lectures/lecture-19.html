Scaling Self-Attention

1.1 The Softmax Saturation Problem

In standard dot-product attention, we calculate similarity scores by taking the dot product of query and key vectors: Q KT. As the dimension of the key vectors dk increases, the magnitude of these dot products grows. When passed through a softmax activation function, large values cause the distribution to saturate, focusing almost all probability mass on a single token. This leads to vanishing gradients during backpropagation because the derivative of the softmax function approaches zero in saturated regions.

1.2 Variance of Dot Products Derivation

To understand why dot products scale with dimension, consider two independent random vectors q and k of size dk, where each element has a mean of 0 and a variance of 1. Their dot product is:

q · k = ∑i=1dk qi ki

Since the components are independent, the mean of the dot product is 0. The variance of each term in the sum is:

Var(qi ki) = Var(qi) Var(ki) = 1 × 1 = 1

Summing over all dk dimensions, the variance of the complete dot product is:

Var(q · k) = ∑i=1dk Var(qi ki) = dk

1.3 The Square-Root Scaling Factor

To pull the variance back to 1 and ensure stable gradients, we scale the dot products by dividing by the square root of the key dimension: 1 / √(dk). This scaling factor keeps the softmax input values within a range that avoids saturation and supports effective optimization.

Why Variance Control Matters

Unscaled attention makes training deep models virtually impossible because softmax saturation leaves the weights unchanged, trapping the model in a state of random representations.

Geometry of Self-Attention

2.1 Spatial Vector Mappings

Self-attention maps tokens from an input feature space to a contextualized semantic space. By projecting word embeddings into Query, Key, and Value vectors, the model forms dynamic spatial representation mappings. High similarity translates to smaller angular separation in high-dimensional vector spaces, aligning tokens that share relationships.

2.2 Dot-Product Projection Angles

The geometric interpretation of the dot product u · v = ||u|| ||v|| cos(θ) defines similarity as the cosine of the angle between projections. When a Query vector projects onto a Key vector, it evaluates how much of the Key's direction is shared. A 90-degree angle (orthogonal) implies zero correlation, yielding a zero attention score prior to scaling and softmax operations.

The "Self" in Self-Attention

3.1 Internal Relationships Modeling

The term self-attention comes from the source of the Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V) matrices. In self-attention, all three matrices are projected from the *same* input sequence. This design allows the model to relate different positions of a single sequence to compute a representation of the same sequence, identifying dependencies like pronoun antecedents internally.

3.2 Self vs. Cross-Attention Interfaces

In contrast to self-attention, cross-attention connects two different sequences (e.g. source and target languages in translation). In cross-attention:

  • The Query matrix (Q) comes from the target sequence (decoder path).
  • The Key (K) and Value (V) matrices are imported from the source sequence (encoder path).

Multi-Head Attention Subspaces

4.1 Parallel Projection Heads

Rather than performing a single attention operation with large 512-dimensional vectors, Multi-Head Attention projects the vectors into multiple lower-dimensional representation subspaces (heads) in parallel. If we use 8 heads, each head processes vectors of size 64 (512 / 8 = 64). Each head can focus on a distinct relationship subspace, tracking local or global contexts separately.

4.2 Concatenation and Dimensional Projection

Once the individual heads compute their attention outputs, they are concatenated back together to reconstruct a single matrix of dimension N × 512. This concatenated output is then multiplied by a final projection weight matrix WO to mix information across all heads and output a unified representation.

Positional Encoding Rationale

5.1 Permutation Invariance of Attention

The math of dot-product attention is completely order-independent: if you shuffle the rows of the input sequence, the output vectors will be shuffled in the exact same way, but their content values will remain unchanged. This permutation invariance means the model treats "The dog chased the cat" and "The cat chased the dog" identically in terms of word relationships.

5.2 Breaking Symmetry

To incorporate sequence order, we must break this permutation symmetry. Positional encodings do this by adding unique, position-dependent vectors to the word embeddings before the self-attention blocks, enabling the model to tell the difference between positions 1, 2, and 100.
