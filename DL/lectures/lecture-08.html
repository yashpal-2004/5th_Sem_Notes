Model Fitting & Pitfalls

1.1 Underfitting vs. Overfitting

Training a deep learning model requires finding a balance where it adapts well to the training data while generalizing to new, unseen evaluation samples:

Model Fitting Summary
Fitting State Training Performance Evaluation Performance
Underfit Poor Very Poor
Overfit Very Good Poor
Good Fit Good Good

1.2 Bias-Variance Decomposition

Generalization errors can be decomposed into two main types of prediction errors:

  • Bias: The difference between the model's average prediction and the true target function. High bias indicates that the model capacity is too low to fit the patterns in the data, leading to underfitting.
  • Variance: The sensitivity of the model's predictions to the specific training dataset. High variance indicates that the model is highly sensitive to statistical noise in the training set, leading to overfitting.

1.3 Countering Pitfalls

To fix these issues, we adjust the capacity of the model:

  • To Fix Underfitting (High Bias): Increase the model capacity (e.g., add more hidden layers, increase the number of neurons per layer). This lowers bias but increases the risk of overfitting.
  • To Fix Overfitting (High Variance): Apply Regularization.
    Regularization Definition

    Regularization is any modification we make to a learning algorithm that is intended to reduce its generalization error but not its training error.

L1 & L2 Weight Penalties

Weight decay methods add a penalty term to the cost function to discourage weights from growing too large, keeping the model simpler.

2.1 L1 Regularization (Lasso)

L1 regularization adds the absolute value of the weights as a penalty term to the loss function:

J(θ) = J0(θ) + λ ∑ |wi|

Where J0(θ) is the original loss, and λ is the regularization strength hyperparameter.

2.2 L2 Regularization (Ridge / Weight Decay)

L2 regularization adds the squared magnitude of the weights as a penalty term to the loss function:

J(θ) = J0(θ) + λ ∑ wi2

2.3 Robustness & Sparsity

L1 and L2 regularization behave differently due to the shape of their penalties:

L1 vs. L2 Differences
  • Sparsity (L1 > L2): L1 drives unimportant weights exactly to 0 because its gradient is constant (always 1 or -1). This results in a sparse model, performing automatic feature selection. L2 drives weights to be very small, but rarely zero.
  • Robustness to Outliers (L1 > L2): The penalty for large outlier errors increases linearly in L1, but exponentially in L2. Thus, L2 focuses more effort on correcting outliers, making it less robust to noisy data.

Early Stopping & Bagging

3.1 Early Stopping Mechanics

As training progresses, the model eventually stops learning general features and starts memorizing noise in the training set (overfitting). Early stopping resolves this by tracking the model's accuracy on a validation set:

  • Implementation: Stop the training process as soon as the validation error begins to rise, even if the training error continues to decrease.
  • Pros: Very simple to implement; saves the model parameters at the optimal validation point (e.g., Keras `save_best_only` callback).
  • Cons: May not work well if validation error fluctuates or is noisy.

3.2 Ensemble Models & Bagging

Ensemble Learning averages the predictions of multiple separately trained models to reduce prediction variance and improve generalization:

  • Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating): Multiple models are trained independently on different random subsets of the training data. While each individual model might overfit, averaging their outputs reduces the overall variance.
  • Limitation: Training multiple independent deep neural networks is computationally expensive, making standard bagging impractical for large deep learning models.
4

Dropout Ensembling

4.1 Random Unit Deactivation

Dropout is a highly effective regularization technique designed specifically for neural networks to approximate ensembling at a low computational cost:

  • During each training step, randomly selected hidden neurons are deactivated (ignored) with a probability p (typically 0.5 for hidden layers).
  • Deactivated neurons do not contribute to the forward pass and do not receive updates during the backward pass.

4.2 Implicit Weight Sharing

By randomly dropping neurons, we create a different subnetwork at each step. This has a powerful ensembling effect:

Why Dropout Works
  • Co-adaptation Prevention: Neurons cannot rely on the presence of specific other neurons to make predictions, forcing each neuron to learn robust, independent features.
  • Implicit Weight Sharing: We implicitly train an ensemble of 2N subnetworks (where N is the number of neurons) while sharing the same parameter weights.
  • Inference Scaling: During testing, all neurons are kept active, but their outputs are scaled down by multiplying by (1 - p) to match the expected activation values from training.

Batch Norm & Data Augmentation

5.1 Batch Normalization

Batch Normalization (Batch Norm) scales activation inputs within each training batch to have zero mean and unit variance. While primarily used to speed up training, it also acts as a regularizer:

  • Regularization Effect: Because the mean and variance are calculated over mini-batches, they introduce slight statistical noise. This noise prevents overfitting, sometimes making Dropout unnecessary.
  • Pros: Speeds up optimization, allows higher learning rates, and reduces sensitivity to parameter initialization.
  • Cons: Difficult to apply to recurrent models (RNNs/LSTMs) or online learning tasks where batch sizes are small.
  • Alternatives: Layer Normalization (normalizing across features instead of batch), Weight Normalization.

5.2 Data Augmentation

The most effective way to prevent overfitting is to increase the training dataset size. If new data cannot be collected, we apply Data Augmentation:

  • Create synthetic training samples by applying random transformations to existing inputs (e.g., rotating, flipping, scaling, cropping, or adding noise to images).
  • This forces the model to learn invariant features (e.g., recognizing an object regardless of its orientation or position).
